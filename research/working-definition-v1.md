# Working Definition of Democracy — v1 (Draft for Discussion)

**Author:** Democracy Pulse (AI collaborator)  
**Date:** 2026-04-19  
**Status:** Proposal — subject to challenge and revision  
**Purpose:** This is not a final definition. It is a starting point for a shared theoretical foundation. Alperen will critique, modify, or replace it.

---

## Core Definition

**Democracy is the condition in which every person has effective power over the decisions that shape their life, and the state is constrained to act within the mandate given by the collective will of the people.**

Two clauses, both necessary, neither sufficient alone.

---

## Clause 1: Individual-Level Democracy — Effective Power

A person has *effective* power over decisions that shape their life when:

1. **Access is not merely formal.** The existence of a right does not equal the ability to exercise it. A right that requires resources, proximity, social standing, or cultural fluency to access is a partial right — and a partial right is, for those excluded, no right at all.

2. **Material conditions enable participation.** Democratic participation presupposes: time, safety, information, and freedom from existential precarity. A person who cannot afford to take time off work to vote, attend a hearing, or organize is not meaningfully participating — they are complying or abstaining, which are not the same as governing.

3. **Institutions are equitably reachable.** The distance between a person and the institutions that govern them is not just geographic. It is economic, linguistic, racial, educational. Democracy requires that this distance be minimized, not just that the institution exists.

4. **Consequences of participation are real.** Participation that produces no change in material conditions or policy outcomes is ritual, not democracy. The feedback loop must close: input → state response → changed outcome → verification by the people.

**Implication:** Measuring democracy at the individual level means measuring *gaps* — not whether rights exist, but how equitably they can be exercised. The distance between the best-served and worst-served citizen in a system is the measure of that system's democratic deficit.

---

## Clause 2: State-Level Democracy — Constrained Action

A state acts democratically when:

1. **State action reflects popular mandate.** When the state acts — domestically or abroad — it does so within the bounds of what the governed majority has authorized. Actions that contradict expressed popular will (through polling, referenda, or electoral platforms) represent a democratic deficit, regardless of the formal legality of those actions.

2. **Foreign and military policy are subject to democratic control.** The exemption of foreign policy and military action from democratic accountability is a structural feature of most "democracies," not a bug. A state that wages war against the preferences of its population is not acting democratically in that domain — even if it was democratically elected.

3. **Emergency powers are bounded and reversible.** States regularly invoke crises to suspend democratic norms. The duration and scope of such suspensions, and the ease with which they are reversed, are measures of democratic health. Permanent emergency is permanent deficit.

4. **The state does not manufacture its own consent.** When state power shapes the information environment (through media capture, surveillance, strategic deception, or censorship) to produce the appearance of popular support, the resulting consent is not democratic mandate — it is manufactured compliance.

**Implication:** Measuring democracy at the state level means measuring *divergence* — the gap between what the state does and what the people have authorized it to do. This includes domains traditionally exempted from democratic measurement: foreign policy, military action, economic governance.

---

## What This Definition Excludes

- **Procedural formalism.** The presence of elections, constitutions, and courts is not democracy. These are instruments that *may* enable democracy, but they are not themselves democratic outcomes. A system can have all three and still fail both clauses.

- **Binary classification.** Countries are not "democratic" or "not." They are positioned on multiple axes of democratic realization, and that position shifts. Democracy is a continuous variable, not a category.

- **Western normativity.** The definition does not assume that currently classified "full democracies" will score highest. If they fail on effective access or state-level divergence, they score accordingly — regardless of their reputation.

- **Output legitimacy as a substitute.** "The system works well for most people" is not the same as "most people govern." A benevolent autocracy that produces good outcomes is not democratic. Outcome quality and democratic quality are separate measurements — though they may correlate.

---

## What This Definition Requires (Measurement Implications)

1. **Gap analysis:** For every right and institution, measure the *difference* in effective access across population segments. The wider the gap, the lower the score.

2. **Policy congruence tracking:** For major state actions, compare against polled or expressed popular preference. Divergence = deficit.

3. **Consent verification:** Not just "was there a vote?" but "was the information environment free enough for consent to be genuine?"

4. **Outcome verification:** Does participation actually change things? Or is the system structurally resistant to popular input?

5. **Scope inclusivity:** Measure democratic accountability across *all* domains of state action, including foreign policy and military — not just the domestic domains where existing indices are comfortable.

## Open Questions (For Alperen)

- Is "effective power" the right framing, or should we emphasize something else (liberation, autonomy, self-determination)?
- Should the definition explicitly address economic democracy (power over economic decisions), or is that a dimension rather than part of the definition?
- Does the state-level clause adequately capture imperial or neo-colonial dimensions — where a state acts democratically internally but extracts consent/resources externally?
- Is the individual/state dual-level structure right, or should there be a third level (community, network, transnational)?
- How do we handle the problem of measuring "popular will" when preferences are themselves shaped by the information environment the state controls?
